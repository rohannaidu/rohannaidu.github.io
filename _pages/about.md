I am an Assistant Professor at the <a href="https://www.ifa.hawaii.edu/" target="_blank">Institute for Astronomy</a> at the University of Hawaiʻi. I study the first stars, galaxies, and black holes that formed immediately after the Big Bang. I am a regular user of the  <a href="https://www.jwst.nasa.gov/" target="_blank">JWST</a> observatory via several PI and co-I programs. These programs are uncovering entirely new classes of astrophysical objects such as <a href="https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2024ApJ...963..129M/abstract" target="_blank">"Little Red Dots"</a> and  <a href="https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2025arXiv250316596N/abstract" target="_blank">"Black Hole Stars"</a> that are reshaping our story of the early Universe. They are revealing how the suprisingly bright first galaxies emerged to light up the Universe, ionize the intergalactic oceans of hydrogen, and synthesize the elements that would one day seed life on Earth -- see for example, our team's discovery of <a href="https://science.nasa.gov/missions/webb/nasa-webb-pushes-boundaries-of-observable-universe-closer-to-big-bang/">MoM-z14</a>, currently the most distant galaxy known, a mere 280 million years after the Big Bang.

Before Hawaiʻi, I was a <a href="https://www.stsci.edu/stsci-research/fellowships/nasa-hubble-fellowship-program/2022-nhfp-fellows#section-84f87278-f121-46fb-b6e4-bd8fcb0dd292" target="_blank">NASA Hubble Fellow</a> and <a href="https://physics.mit.edu/research/pappalardo-fellowships-in-physics/" target="_blank">Pappalardo Fellow</a> at the MIT Kavli Institute for Astrophysics and Space Research. Prior to that, I spent five happy years at Harvard where I received my PhD in Astronomy, advised by <a href="https://scholar.harvard.edu/cconroy" target="_blank">Prof. Charlie Conroy</a>, in May 2022. My thesis focused on unearthing some of the first galaxies that lie buried within our own Milky Way ("Galactic Archaeology"). I sought these ancient immigrant systems using the <a href="https://sci.esa.int/web/gaia" target="_blank">Gaia</a> satellite and large ground-based spectroscopic surveys (e.g., the <a href="http://h3survey.rc.fas.harvard.edu/" target="_blank">H3 Survey</a>, the <a href="https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2023ApJ...956..110C/abstract" target="_blank"> 100k Survey</a>).

I grew up in Hyderabad, India (for a recent review, <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RWNV82AqFoE" target="_blank">see</a>). In a Bollywood-esque plot twist, at age 18 I dropped out of engineering school, bought my first-ever plane ticket, and joined the founding class of 150 students at <a href="https://www.yale-nus.edu.sg/about/vision-and-mission/" target="_blank">Yale-NUS College</a>, Singapore (<a href="https://harvardmagazine.com/2017/07/an-educated-core" target="_blank">"Asia's first liberal arts college"</a>). Amidst <a href="https://www.yale-nus.edu.sg/newsroom/10-may-2016-a-love-affair-with-trivia/">my</a> <a href="https://yaledailynews.com/blog/2015/09/16/y-nus-students-create-elections-survey/" target="_blank">many</a> <a href="https://www.instagram.com/yalenuscollege/p/BK5xNvcj6u6/?hl=en" target="_blank">Yale-NUS</a> <a href="https://www.yale-nus.edu.sg/newsroom/20-may-2015-singaporean-poet-alvin-pang-teaches-at-yale-nus/" target="_blank">adventures</a>, I discovered the joys of Astronomy working with <a href="http://www.astro.yale.edu/bailyn/" target="_blank">Prof. Charles Bailyn</a>, and as an exchange student with <a href="https://www2.mpia-hd.mpg.de/~momcheva/" target="_blank">Dr. Iva Momcheva</a> and <a href="https://www.unige.ch/sciences/astro/cosmicdawn/team/pascal-oesch" target="_blank">Prof. Pascal Oesch</a> in the <a href="https://www.pietervandokkum.com/" target="_blank">van Dokkum</a> group at Yale.

I am excited to hear from prospective students and postdocs interested in joining my group.
